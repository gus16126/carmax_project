August 2024.
In August 2024, after being reassigned to the home delivery team, I conducted a thorough inspection of the two-car home delivery truck. During this inspection, I identified hairline cracks in the welding on the upper deck of the truck. These cracks were found beneath the upper deck extensions, and both sides showed signs of damage.

Currently, these are just small hairline cracks, but if left unaddressed, they could compromise safety over time. Upon examining the potential causes of this damage, I concluded that excessive downward pressure is being applied during the loading and unloading process. This pressure occurs between the tips of the upper deck extensions and the lower deck when operators load and unload vehicles.

The situation worsens when a larger vehicle, such as a four-door Ram 2500, is loaded. In these cases, the weight of the diesel engine rests directly on the areas affected by the cracks, exacerbating the issue due to the additional downward pressure from the hydraulic system.

To prevent further damage, I have implemented a procedure where I pin the upper deck hydraulic in the second security hole from top to bottom during loading and unloading. This action creates an inch-and-a-half separation between the upper deck and the lower deck, allowing for safe operations without interference during the loading and unloading processes.

By following my procedures, or similar protocols established by my logistics manager, future repairs and safety issues can be prevented. I have shared this information with several members of the home delivery team, as well as with my logistics manager, Matthew Douglas.

By taking the time to learn their procedures, respecting everyone's time, and establishing relationships with managers and employees, I was able to turn a challenging situation into a manageable one.

February 2025.
While I was assigned as a driver for the four-car rollback, I occasionally operated the home delivery two-car carrier during maintenance and repairs on the rollback. From my first time using the home delivery two-car carrier, I noticed that the strap and ratchet combination setup was incorrect. 

It became clear to me that the strap spool was designed to move from one side of the ratchet shaft to the other, ensuring that the strap was in a direct line with the tire being tied down, as required by Cottrell. However, the way it was being used obstructed the ratchet's functionality and compromised safety. Additionally, I observed wear and tear on the straps, caused by the strap going over the edges of the ratchet spool and onto the ratchet shaft, creating stress points that further compromised safety.

I initially voiced my concerns to the drivers and my lead driver at the time, Luis Rejon, but it seemed that a decision had already been made to continue using the system as it was. 

After being reassigned to the home delivery team in August 2024, I noticed the same concerning issues with the strap and ratchet setup again. 
I decided to seek information that would clarify the correct usage of the system. Upon researching the matter, I discovered that the two-car delivery truck features Cottrell's QuixSpinz Ratchet System, which had indeed been used incorrectly, and my prior conclusions were correct.

In an effort to improve safety during the operation of our two-car trucks, I shared this information with as many members of the home delivery team as possible. However, I did not see any notable impact from my efforts. 

To engage team members who had the authority to influence the implementation of the correct procedure, I decided in February 2025 to demonstrate the proper use of the ratchets to the Sr. fleet drivers at store 7110 and my logistics manager, Matthew Douglas. 
Safety could be significantly improved if the correct procedures are implemented in the near future.
